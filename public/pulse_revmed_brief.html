Intelligence Brief Daraxonrasib · 2L PDAC June 21, 2026 · 7:00 AM ET

The ASCO 2026 plenary delivered what PDAC KOLs are calling "unprecedented" OS and PFS results for daraxonrasib. The adoption race is now on — and the bottlenecks are real.

RASolute 302 delivered concordant OS and PFS in 2L RAS-mutant PDAC — the first Phase 3 trial in this setting to hit both primary endpoints. Post-ASCO KOL signal velocity is at a historic peak. But three forces are slowing translation to practice: RAS testing infrastructure gaps in community oncology, Borghaei-led skepticism in NSCLC creating cross-indication noise, and payer conversations that have not yet absorbed the clinical benefit narrative. The window to shape the adoption curve is now.

02Community Oncology · Adoption Bottleneck

Academic centers are ready. Community oncology is not. ~40% of sites lack comprehensive RAS profiling — and they know it.

↑ Accelerating▲ Adoption Risk

MSL field reports from four regions converge on the same finding: community oncologists are enthusiastic about RASolute 302, but approximately 40% of community sites do not have NGS panels that cover all RAS variants beyond KRAS G12C. Physicians are identifying KRAS G12C-positive patients but missing G12D, G12V, and other RAS-mutant patients who would also qualify. The testing gap is not a will problem — it is an infrastructure problem. Comprehensive RAS/NGS testing requires (1) a panel that covers all RAS variants, (2) a CDx workflow that is reimbursed, and (3) local laboratory or send-out capability. All three are inconsistent in community settings today.

▲ Adoption Risk

If the RAS testing gap is not addressed before NDA filing, daraxonrasib's label will describe a patient population that community oncologists cannot reliably identify. Every month of delay in testing infrastructure is a month of patients being missed.

01Testing Infrastructure Gap

The problem: community oncologists are testing for KRAS G12C only. Daraxonrasib's label population is all RAS mutations.

↑ Prioritised▲ Adoption Risk

RASolute 302 enrolled patients with RAS-mutant PDAC — a population that includes KRAS G12D (~33% of PDAC), KRAS G12V (~21%), KRAS G12C (~2%), and other variants. Standard community oncology reflex testing often covers KRAS G12C (driven by sotorasib/adagrasib NSCLC) but may not include a full RAS variant panel. The practical result: a patient with KRAS G12D PDAC who would qualify for daraxonrasib may never be identified because the G12D mutation was not on the panel ordered. Comprehensive RAS/NGS testing infrastructure — with CDx reimbursement confirmation — must be deployed to community oncology settings before commercial launch to avoid systemic patient identification failures.

Intelligence Brief Elironrasib + Daraxonrasib June 21, 2026 · 7:00 AM ET

Pasi Jänne is building the translational case for the RAS(ON) doublet. Resistance mechanisms for G12C-selective agents are the scientific anchor — and the opportunity is real.

Pasi Jänne (Dana-Farber) has emerged as the leading scientific voice for the RAS(ON) doublet strategy — combining elironrasib (KRAS G12C-selective) with daraxonrasib (multi-selective) to address acquired resistance to G12C-selective monotherapy. His translational perspective at ASCO 2026 and a LinkedIn post on RAS(ON) doublet combination rationale have seeded academic thoracic oncology discussion. This is the scientific anchor for the multi-selective + G12C-selective combination narrative, and it is the most credible counter-narrative to Borghaei's G12C-selective skepticism.

01Translational Science · Combination Rationale

Resistance to G12C-selective agents creates the scientific case for the RAS(ON) doublet. Jänne's data is the anchor.

● EmergingTranslational Signal

KRAS G12C resistance mechanisms following sotorasib or adagrasib include co-mutation acquisition and on-target KRAS amplification — both of which may be sensitive to the multi-selective RAS(ON) approach. The translational rationale: G12C-selective agents target the inactive (OFF) conformation of KRAS G12C; daraxonrasib targets the active (ON) conformation across KRAS variants; combining the two covers both conformational states and additional mutation variants. Jänne's lab has published preclinical and translational data supporting this approach. The clinical strategy — elironrasib + daraxonrasib — is currently in early clinical development.

"The RAS(ON) doublet approach — targeting both the OFF and ON conformational states across KRAS variants — is scientifically compelling as a resistance strategy. The question is whether the translational rationale holds in early clinical data."

— Pasi Jänne, MD PhD · LinkedIn · June 2026 (paraphrased from public post)

Payer engagement should begin this quarter with early P&T committee medical affairs conversations.
